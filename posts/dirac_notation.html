Dirac's bra-ket notation

It is very difficult to find notation that is concise, correct and understandable by at least two people.
--some great man

Example 1:
Suppose,

$|\Psi\rangle = \Sigma_i \alpha_i |i \rangle$

$|\Phi\rangle = \Sigma_i \beta_i |i \rangle$

$\langle \Psi | \Phi \rangle = \Sigma_i \alpha_i^{*} \beta_i$ ...(1)

Now,

$|\Psi \rangle = \Psi(x)$

$|\Phi \rangle = \Phi(x)$

$\langle \Psi | \Phi \rangle = \int \Psi(x)^{*} \Phi(x) dx$ ...(2)

Look at the left sides of equations (1) and (2).
That means this notation abstracts away differences
between discrete and continuous, finite dimensions and infinite dimensions.

Example 2:

Theorem: Orthogonal transformations preserve lengths and angles.

If $O$ is an orthogonal matrix then $O^T = O^{-1}$.

$|x\rangle \rightarrow O|x\rangle$

$|y\rangle \rightarrow O|y\rangle$

$\langle x|y \rangle \rightarrow \langle x|O^T O|y\rangle = \langle x|O^{-1} O|y\rangle = \langle x|y \rangle$

Look up proof of the same theorem on the internet.
See how it is given using usual notation and you will bow to Mr. Dirac.
I think Linear Algebra should be taught using Dirac's bra-ket notation.
